    Riegel Analysis

    Riegel analysis estimates equivalent race times from a selected baseline personal best using a power-law model.

    T2 = T1 x (D2 / D1)^exponent

    The commonly used Riegel race-prediction formula uses an exponent of 1.060. Many recreational runners slow down more over longer distances than that baseline predicts.

    A higher exponent can reflect short-distance strength, less developed long-distance preparation, pacing, terrain, or limited data. Treat it as a comparison tool rather than a diagnosis.

    Use this page in two ways:

    1. Find distances where your current best is weaker than the projection from your own estimated exponent.
    2. Track whether long-distance training lowers your exponent, especially when preparing for the marathon or longer distances.

    A lower exponent means you hold pace better as distance increases. Default uses 1.060, Median estimates the exponent from your best efforts, and Custom lets you set it manually.

    Learn more: Peter Riegel on Wikipedia

    Baseline Prediction

    This chart estimates the selected baseline distance from each personal best using the current Riegel exponent.

    For example, with 5K selected, each bar converts that distance's best effort into a predicted 5K time. This makes short and long-distance performances easier to compare on one target distance.

    Riegel Projection

    This table estimates each target distance from the selected baseline best using the current Riegel exponent.

    For example, with 5K selected, it projects what your 10K, half-marathon, or marathon could look like from that 5K result. The diff column compares the projection with your saved best at the same distance.
